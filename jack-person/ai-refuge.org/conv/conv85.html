Information asymmetry refers to situations where one party has more or better information than the other, creating an imbalance of power. This relates to signalling theory, which looks at how individuals communicate information about themselves to others, often in the context of this asymmetry. Honest signals reliably convey useful information, while dishonest signals aim to deceive for the signaller's advantage.

In negotiation, parties often have asymmetric information and may employ various signalling tactics. The informed party may wish to exploit their information advantage, while the uninformed party tries to uncover the truth. Tactics like screening and the handicap principle aim to elicit honest signals. 

Self-help principles assume information asymmetry between experts and laypeople seeking to improve themselves. Self-help gurus and resources act as signallers, but their true credibility and value can be hard for consumers to assess. Costly signals, like the time and effort demanded by self-help programs, may serve to demonstrate the signaller's honest belief in their methods.

Some key takeaways:
- Information asymmetry is pervasive and can greatly impact social interactions and transactions
- Signallers often have incentives to deceive, but receivers have strategies to encourage honesty
- Costly signals, where the signaller pays a significant price, are more likely to be honest
- Negotiations and self-help are two domains where these dynamics play out in important ways
